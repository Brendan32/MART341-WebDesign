How to Make a Simple Conlang with Substitution

Are you writing a story and want an easy-to-create language for your fictional people? Do you want a coded speech that you could easily translate and easily speak? Just want to dabble in conlanging for fun? Then this Instructable is just for you! I'll explain how to easily create a conlang using a substitution cipher, with no need to write a whole dictionary.

  1. What Do You Want?
  2. The Vowels
  3. The Consonants
  4. Editing

Step One: What Do You Want?

First, and most importantly, you should start by thinking about what you want your result to be. Do you want something fantasy-like? Something menacing? Something pleasant? This will greatly influence your decisions as you go, especially in step 4. Something that often helps, though it isn't necessary, is taking a word you have in mind and changing the letters to what you'd like to see (ex. "heart" to "suyld"). You can then take this and apply it to other words, and work from there. Below I provide a table to illustrate the changes that occur with each step. Bear in mind: you do not need to follow my example to the letter. This is simply to guide you. Make your creation your own!

Samples Results
I walked to the park. I walked to the park.
He ate one cookie. He ate one cookie.
She stands straight. She stands straight.
The dogs are barking. The dogs are barking.

Step Two: The Vowels

The best method is to restrict yourself to substituting vowels for other vowels. If you substitute vowels for consonants, pronouncing your words can get hairy pretty quickly (just think what would happen if I substituted A for T: tpple, ctp, ptn, etc). But, wait a minute, why did I include the letter Y? Well, fun fact, the letter Y is much more often a vowel than a consonant. Plus, if we make Y a vowel, the places where it is a consonant won't be heavily affected.

So, for my example, I'll just make it simple and reverse them. A is Y, E is U, I is O, and all vice versa. Here, you can choose yourself what is what, and you can easily experiment and change it later.

Samples Results
I walked to the park. O wylkud ti thu pyrk.
He ate one cookie. Hu ytu inu ciikou.
She stands straight. Shu stynds stryoght.
The dogs are barking. Thu digs yru byrkong.

Not much yet, but we've only just started!

Step Three: The Consonants

Since there are many more of them, the consonants will naturally influence your result far more than the vowels. You have very few restrictions here. You don't even have to do a strict 1-to-1 substitution. You could make three different consonants become one, or introduce completely new consonants to use. Generally, do what sounds right to you! Anything that comes out weird or unpronouncable can be revised later.

So, for this I'll get a little more complicated. All the voiceless consonants with voiced counterparts will switch to the voiced counterparts, and vice versa. B is P, D is T, F is V, G is K, S is Z, and all vice versa. What about C, H, J, L, M, N, Q, R, W, and X? Let's say, C is G, H is S, J is T, L is R and vice versa, M is N and vice versa, Q is K, W is L, and X is F. That should give us something to work with, if not a good enough result to be used.

Samples Results
I walked to the park. O lyrgut di dsu bylg.
He ate one cookie. Su ydu imu giigou.
She stands straight. Zsu zdymtz zdlyoksd.
The dogs are barking. Dsu tikz ylu pylgomk.

Well, it's certainly unrecognizable from the original English. However, some of it might do with some tweaking, by the looks of it.

Step Four: Editing

Now that you have all the letters jumbled up into a new form, you may have a few problematic results. Personally, I'm not sure I'd enjoy trying to pronounce "zdlyoksd." So, here you can change what letters become what, but you can also start making rules. Rules are essentially addendums to the substitutions that can take plenty of different forms. Collapsing clusters of letters, changing the substitutions in certain conditions, moving letters in a word around, etc. These can help to simplify words and closer approximate what you want from the code.

So, I'm going to make a rule that vowel clusters will collapse into whichever vowel is first in the word. I'll also make a rule that m must switch to n if preceding k, g, t, or d, and n must switch to m if preceding b or p. Finally, I'll make a rule that any consonant clusters larger than two letters in a root word must drop all but the first two consonants. This all might seem complicated, but it'll make things much simpler.

Samples Results
I walked to the park. O lyrgut di dsu bylg.
He ate one cookie. Su ydu imu gigo.
She stands straight. Zsu zdyntz zdyks.
The dogs are barking. Dsu tikz ylu pylgonk.

Now that I'm finished with it for the purposes of this guide, I will show a full paragraph as an example.

An elephant is the biggest living animal on land. It is quite huge in size. It is usually black or grey in colour. Elephants have four legs, a long trunk and two white tusks near their trunk. Apart from this, they have two big ears and a short tail. Elephants are vegetarian. They eat all kinds of plants especially bananas. They are quite social, intelligent and useful animals. They are used to carry logs of wood from one place to another. They are good swimmers.
Ym urubsynd oz dsu pokkuzd rofonk ymonyr im rynd. Od oz kedu seku om zosu. Od oz ezerra prygg il klu om giril. Urubsyndz syfu vil rukz, y rink dleng ynt dli lsotu dezgz mul dsul dleng. Ybyld vlin dsoz, dsu syfu dli pok ulz ynt y zsild dyr. Urubsyndz ylu fukudylom. Dsu ud yrr gontz iv bryndz uzbugorra pymymyz. Dsu ylu kedu zigor, ondurrokund ynt ezuver ymonyr. Dsu ylu ezut di gylla rikz iv lit vlin imu brygu di ymidsul. Dsu ylu kit zlonnulz.

Perhaps it could use some more work, but overall it serves its purpose pretty well! If yours isn't what you wanted, continue revising, creating rules, whatever you need to get it to what you want. It's yours to mold and shape, so do what you want with it!
